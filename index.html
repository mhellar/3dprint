3D printing, also known as additive manufacturing(am), refers to processes used to create a three-dimensional object in which layers of material are formed under computer control to create a physical object.

 Objects can be of almost any shape or geometry and are produced using digital model data from a 3D model

3D printing is achieved using an additive process, where succesive layers of material are laid down in different shapes.

Subtractive Manufacturing vs Addtive Manufacturing
    3D Printing has been around for decades
  • It was invented by Chuck Hull who created the first 3D printer protoype in 1994
  • He named the technique Stereo Lithography and patented it in 1986
  • Hull's original design used a UV laser to cure layers of UV sensitive resin
  • By the end of the 1980s similar (AM)technologies such as Fused Deposition Modeling(FDM) and Selective laser Sintering(SLS) were introduced. (More on these later)
In 1996 The "Genisys" from the Stratasys corp, "Actua 2100" from 3d Systems and the "Z402" from the Z corp. were introduced as 3D printing solutions for the industrial market
The price range of these printers was hundred of thousands of dollars.
Over the next 10 years competition between companies led to a price war. In 2007 3D systems released the Vflash 3d printer for $9,900

The holy grail of the industry at the time was to produce a 3D printer under $5000.00 to increase the market share for the technology
Meanwhile in 2005 Dr Adrian Bowyer, a Senior Lecturer in mechanical engineering at the University of Bath founded the RepRap project.
  • In 2011 the BfB RapMan 3D printer based on the designs of the RepRap project went on sale in Kit form for $1433
  • Inspired by the reprap project Makerbot Industries, began seeling open source 3d printers shortly after.
  • The RepRap phenomenon had given rise to a whole new sector of commercial, affordable 3D printers
    • 2012 was the year that alternative 3D printing processes were introduced at the entry level of the market. The B9Creator (utilised DLP technology) and the Form1 (utilised stereolithography)
